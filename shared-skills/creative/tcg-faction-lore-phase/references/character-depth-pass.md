# Character Depth Pass Methodology

Applied after the faction bible and crew structure are locked. Takes named characters from "cardboard cutout" to "lived-in person." Joe direction on personality and motivation should already exist (from lore review sessions) before the depth pass begins.

## Structure per Character

### Surface (Locked)
Bullet list of everything already locked from the faction bible and Joe's design decisions. Don't re-litigate. This section is read-only — it's the foundation the depth pass builds on.

### Origin
Where they came from. Not their whole life — the moment or period that explains who they became. Usually one specific scene or situation.

Good origins answer: "Why this person, in this faction, at this tier?"
Bad origins answer: "Born in X, went to school in Y, joined in Z." — that's a resume, not a story.

### The Moment That Made Them (optional, for major characters)
If there's a specific crystallizing moment that defines the character — Gerald walking into the Gratiot diner in 1935, Lark's six weeks undercover in the Faceless church — give it its own subsection. These are the beats that become card art, flavor text, and legendary card concepts.

### Personality
How they think. How they present. Their internal logic. What they believe about themselves that might not be true. The gap between how they're perceived and who they are.

### What Keeps Them Up at Night
The thing they don't tell anyone. Could be a fear, a regret, a secret, a countdown. This is what makes a character feel real — not their job, but what they think about at 3am when the job can't distract them.

### Key Relationships
Short subsections per significant relationship. Name the relationship ("Father, mentor, ghost"), then the dynamic. Don't list every person they know — only the ones that shape them.

### Day-to-Day
A clock-driven walk through their typical day. Times, locations, actions. Grounds the character in the faction's rhythm. Should cross-reference with the faction bible's daily-life vignettes.

### Voice
How they talk. Include one or two quoted lines that capture their register. These are flavor text candidates.

### Card Hooks
3-4 mechanical concepts that express the character on the table. Not full card designs — directional hooks a designer could build from. Each hook gets a one-line flavor justification.

## Depth Tiers

Not every character gets the full treatment:

| Depth Tier | Who Gets It | Sections |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| **Full** | Leadership, heir apparent, primary antagonist | All sections including optional "Moment That Made Them" |
| **Standard** | Named characters with significant faction roles | All sections except optional |
| **Broad Stroke** | Ensemble characters (Court members, support network) | Surface, Origin, Personality, Role on [tier], Card Hooks — more compact |

Joe will specify which characters need which depth. Default: leadership gets full, named operators get standard, ensemble gets broad stroke.

## Pitfalls

- **Don't invent Joe decisions.** The depth pass builds on locked direction from lore review sessions. If Joe hasn't decided personality type, motivation, or relationship nature yet, that character stays in OPEN — don't guess.
- **Don't write a novel.** The depth pass is for card designers and flavor text writers. If a section runs over 200 words, it's too long. The "What Keeps Them Up at Night" section should be tight enough to fit on a card back.
- **Don't skip the card hooks.** Every character depth pass should produce mechanical ideas. If you can't think of card hooks, the character might not have enough texture yet.
- **Don't make everyone tragic.** Not every character needs a dark secret or a trauma. Vera Nash's defining trait is that she's well-adjusted — and that's what makes her interesting in a faction of killers. Sometimes the most interesting thing about a character is what they DON'T carry.
- **Cross-reference relationships.** When Character A's depth pass mentions Character B, Character B's depth pass should reflect the same relationship from their perspective. Lucy sees Vera as "professional respect, not friendship." Vera sees Lucy as "different species." Both are true. Neither contradicts. The asymmetry IS the texture.
