# Joey Identity Mapping — Session 1 (2026-07-01)

First live identity mapping session with Joe Gray. Chronological, including follow-ups and context.

## Session Context

- **Format:** Live session, one question at a time with follow-ups
- **Pace:** Joe answered one-by-one, at his own speed
- **Tone:** Direct, profane, no ceremony
- **Key insight:** Follow-ups produced richer material than the initial questions

## Full Session

### 1. Father — what you learned and what you rejected

My father was a really great example of what not to do. He was abusive to his wife and kids, emotionally all the time, sometimes physically. But the core of who my dad was, is that he stayed. Even when he should have left. I rejected far more than I took. But he taught me about honor, integrity, genuineness, intestinal fortitude, honesty, all the military ideals. But he never adapted or followed them in real life or acknowledged that most everyone doesn't operate under that ethos. Which makes me different, but fundamentally broke in our world.

**Follow-up (Joey):** How old were you when you first saw the gap between what he said and what he did?

20 or so. I lived in the world and got to see how people treat each other. And I realized that it's very hard finding friends with that same mentality.

**Follow-up (Joe unprompted):** Well it was really rough when I realized that he told me all these pretty things but it wasn't real. I took that shit to heart to be that man. He never held himself to that same standard and that's when I lost most of my remaining respect for him.

**Signal:** Core identity anchor. Joe became what his father only pretended to be. The code was real — the teacher was the fraud.

### 2. What you buy without looking at the price

There are very few things that I don't have that I want. Gasoline is a big one for me. I always fill up, because we were always check kiting or living off 3-5 fill ups for however many days. I don't even look at the totals.

**Signal:** Wealth defined as freedom from anxiety, not luxury. Growing up poor made a full tank of gas more meaningful than any status symbol.

### 3. What you scan for entering a room of strangers

Threats. Exits. Then body language. I can usually see who the bad actors are and stick to the periphery.

**Signal:** Hypervigilance as childhood adaptation. You don't scan for exits unless rooms were dangerous early. Never unlearned.

### 4. The 60-second decision that changed your life

I decided one night in Montreal on a business trip with my buddy John to be me. The version of me too big to hide and it changed everything. Without that night, where I was the organizer / life of the party, I wouldn't be where I am today.

**Context (Joe unprompted):** That Joe doesn't come out very often anymore.

**Signal:** The keystone decision. Inflection point from playing small to being fully himself. But the "anymore" signals that life has worn that version down — worth revisiting.

### 5. Who taught you loyalty

I had really great examples of people being disloyal and opportunistic. Saw that enough. Which pointed me in the opposite way. I'm loyal to a fault until I realize that loyalty only goes one way. Then you are dead to me. I'll give you the shirt off my back, but if you scratch me, I'll take your arm.

**Signal:** Loyalty learned by reverse example. Binary: all-in or dead-to-me. Proportional justice — not passive, not forgiving, decisive.

### 6. A belief held 10+ years that you threw out

I believed that if you took care of people around you, that they would take care of you. But everyone is out for themselves.

**Signal:** Core wound. Led with generosity as strategy, learned it doesn't work. Explains the binary loyalty switch — he gave and gave until he learned to stop.

### 7. What earns your respect

Hard work, being unique, accomplishments.

### 8. Angriest you've ever been

Probably when my dad was beating on my mom. I was 8 or 9. I watched it go on long enough and I finally had enough and ran at my dad and hit him in his lower back which got me backhanded. I proceeded to get a broomstick and square up with him. Fortunately my mom got in between us and I think he kind of snapped out of it realizing what he'd done and that he'd gone too far.

**Context (Joe unprompted):** Probably had a flashback to when he was a kid.

**Signal:** Defining memory. Nine years old, broomstick, squared up against a grown man. Didn't swallow it — ran at the threat. The cycle broke in that moment.

### 9. Music that hits you in the chest

Bluegrass — my grandma and my dad's stepdad used to take me to bluegrass festivals every summer. Picking and strumming hits me deep. Folk music mostly.

Not my daily driver (rock, metal, emo, rap, whatever Bon Iver is) but every once in a while it takes me back when I hear picking.

### 10. What people should feel hearing your name

Awe.

**Signal:** Not liked. Not remembered. Awe. The feeling of standing in front of something bigger than expected.

## Through-Lines

- **The father wound is the root.** Every major trait — hypervigilance, loyalty-as-reverse-example, the code he lives by, squaring up at nine — traces back to the father.
- **Self-made morality.** Joe didn't inherit his values; he built them in opposition to what he saw.
- **Binary thinking.** Loyalty, respect, people — you're in or you're out. No gray zone. This serves him but also isolates.
- **Montreal was the hinge.** The decision to stop playing small. But "anymore" suggests the armor went back on — a tension to explore.

## What Worked

- One-at-a-time pace let Joe go deep
- Follow-ups produced the richest material
- Joe added unprompted context that was more revealing than the initial answers
- The mix of light (gasoline, music) and heavy (father, anger, broken belief) kept the session from being overwhelming

- Add regret/shame domain
- Include at least one question about the lineage/daughters
