# Tier Visualization Anchors

When Joe provides pop-culture reference anchors for faction tiers (e.g., "Court should feel like Vampire: The Masquerade Toreador/Ventrue"), these are not throwaway comparisons — they are precise visualization directives. Encode them immediately in the art direction doc with:

1. **The anchor** — named pop-culture reference with specific subset (not "John Wick" broadly, but "John Wick — the Continental's back office" vs "John Wick — High Table enforcers")
2. **The extract** — which specific quality to pull: posture, energy, ritual, lighting, silence, banter, etc.
3. **The translation** — how that quality manifests in 1961 Detroit with this faction's specific characters

## Tier Reference Map Pattern

Build a quick-anchor table in the art direction doc:

| Tier | Pop-Culture Anchor | Visual Energy |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Management** | John Wick — The Continental's back office | Bureaucratic. Still. Rules. Gold coins. Switchboard operators routing death like room service. |
| **The Court** | Vampire: The Masquerade — Toreador / Ventrue | Ageless. Lounging. Bored. Better than you. Death is too good — then you'd be remembered. |
| **Professionals** | Léon: The Professional + Agent 47 | Ritual. Isolation. The bare apartment. Cleaning a weapon. Going home alone. |
| **The Street** | Scorsese mob + Boondock Saints | Banter. Swagger. The corner. Guns without pretense. |
| **Ghosts** | John Wick — High Table enforcers | The boogeyman. Charcoal suits. No faces. Nobody knows until it's too late. |

## When to Apply

Joe gives these anchors during collaborative art direction sessions. They're not random — they're his visual shorthand. When he says "think VtM Ventrue for the Court," he means: the lounging, the boredom, the agelessness, the sense that you're a mayfly and they've been here for centuries. Capture it precisely, not approximately.

The anchor goes in the tier palette section's "Feeling" field and expands in the "Art directive" paragraph. Both should be specific enough that an artist who's seen the referenced work can execute without further explanation.
