## The Uncertainty of Tomorrow

**Scripture:** "Come now, you who say, 'Today or tomorrow we will go into such and such a town and spend a year there and trade and make a profit'—yet you do not know what tomorrow will bring. What is your life? For you are a mist that appears for a little time and then vanishes." — James 4:13-14 (ESV)

**Stoic:** "The greatest obstacle to living is expectancy, which hangs upon tomorrow and loses today." — Seneca, *On the Shortness of Life*

*Both traditions press the same wound: our confident plans are built on air. James calls it mist; Seneca calls it lost todays.*

**Reflect:**
- When you catch yourself living in "someday" mode — deferring real presence to a future that may never arrive — what specifically pulls you back to the day in front of you?
- Both James and Seneca urge us to hold tomorrow loosely. But isn't some planning an act of love — saving for your children, building something that outlasts you? Where does faithful stewardship end and the arrogance James condemns begin?
- If your life is "a mist," does that make your daily choices *more* weighty or *less*? Why?
