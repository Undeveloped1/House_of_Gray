# The Player's Eye — Art Principles for Card Design

When Joe articulates what card art needs to do for the player, he's defining reusable principles that apply across all factions. Capture them immediately — they're art direction law, not faction-specific texture.

## The Five Principles

### Power Fantasy
Every card should make the player feel something. Power. Control. Cunning. The card they're holding should look like it matters — even a 1-cost runner should radiate the specific confidence of someone who knows their territory better than you know your living room.

### Mystery and Intrigue
Not everything should be obvious on first glance. A figure in the background who might be a Ghost. A classified ad visible over someone's shoulder. A faction identifier (royal blue accent, Villium threading) the player didn't notice the first three times they played the card.

### Cross-Card Alignment
Cards should talk to each other visually. The gun on the gunsmith's bench in one card is the same gun in the hitter's hand in another. The classified ad being typed is the one being read in the next card over. Players who notice these connections should feel like detectives — rewarded for paying attention.

### Re-readability (Easter Egg Principle)
Every card should contain something you don't catch until the third, fifth, or tenth look. A reflection. A shadow where a person should be. A detail that only makes sense once you've read another card's flavor text. The card is never finished — it reveals itself over time. **Joe: "I want people to look at it every single time and see something new."**

### Thumbnail Readability
On a phone screen, the card must read instantly. Silhouette, palette, and focal point do the heavy lifting. The detail work is for the second, third, and tenth look — not the first. **Mobile-first art direction.**

## Compliance Language

Every faction art direction doc should include this as a compliance item:

> **Contains something the player won't catch on first look** — an Easter egg, cross-card reference, background detail, or hidden alignment. The card should reveal itself over time. Ninety percent of players don't see it on the first pass. Ten percent do. One hundred percent feel rewarded when they finally notice.

## When Joe Invokes This

Joe will say things like:
- "We're dealing with card bodies here — they need artwork that is enticing to the user that makes them feel powerful"
- "It needs to be mysterious and intriguing"
- "There needs to be Easter eggs and hidden fun shit"
- "I want people to look at it every single time and see something new"
- "See an alignment as they're playing the game across all of that"

When you hear any of these, the Player's Eye principles are being invoked. Apply all five to the current faction's art direction.
